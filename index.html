About Us

A Detroit Firm Built on Precision and Prestige

Aurum Import Group was founded in Detroit by logistics and compliance professionals with deep roots in international trade. We recognized a gap: fragrance importers needed a partner who understood both the luxury nuance of perfumery and the technical rigor of dangerous goods regulation.

Perfumes and cosmetic fragrances contain alcohol and volatile compounds classified as flammable liquids under IATA, IMDG, and DOT regulations. Our team manages every aspect of this designation from UN3065 and UN1266 classification to proper labeling, packing groups, and shipper's declarations.

Working alongside licensed customs brokers, we ensure your shipments clear U.S. Customs and Border Protection seamlessly, whether arriving via Detroit Metropolitan Wayne County Airport, Port of Detroit, or other points of entry.

How FragranceTok Became the Most Powerful Sales Channel in Perfume

Over the past decade, an unlikely corner of social media has transformed the global fragrance industry. "PerfumeTok", the fragrance community on TikTok, has grown from a niche hobby into one of the most influential forces in beauty marketing.

Beginning in 2019, fragrance enthusiasts started posting short videos reviewing perfumes and ranking their favorite scents. Early creators had relatively small audiences, but within just a few years, leading fragrance influencers grew from a few hundred thousand followers to millions of followers, establishing a new category of online creator.

As these creators gained audiences, the broader PerfumeTok community expanded rapidly. The hashtag #PerfumeTok, which had fewer than one hundred million views in 2019, grew into a multibillion-view ecosystem by the mid-2020s. This surge reflected a shift in how consumers discover fragrance. Unlike traditional perfume marketing built around department stores and magazine ads, PerfumeTok relied on storytelling, aesthetics, and personality-driven recommendations.

The impact on the fragrance industry has been substantial. During the same period that PerfumeTok exploded, the global fragrance market experienced strong growth. Global fragrance sales increased from roughly $48 billion in 2019 to an estimated $78 billion by 2026. While multiple factors contributed to this growth, social media has played an increasingly central role in shaping consumer demand.

PerfumeTok succeeded by solving the unique challenge of selling a sensory product online. Because viewers cannot smell a fragrance through a screen, creators developed descriptive narratives such as describing scents as "rich hotel lobby," "vanilla dessert," or "clean girl aesthetic." These stories made fragrance both entertaining, intriguing, and shareable.

Today, PerfumeTok represents more than a trend. It has reshaped how fragrances are marketed, discovered, and purchased, demonstrating the power of social media to transform even the most traditional consumer industries.

Saks' Collapse Is Closing the One Place Americans Actually Test Perfume

Fragrance is unlike almost any other consumer product you cannot buy it on instinct alone. The decision to spend $200 or $400 on a scent depends entirely on the ability to smell it first, on skin, in a calm and unhurried environment. Saks Fifth Avenue, Neiman Marcus, and Bergdorf Goodman have historically been those environments.

Trained fragrance advisors, extensive brand counters, and a clientele willing to spend made these stores the single most important physical access channel for prestige fragrance in the United States. The Saks Global bankruptcy is dismantling that channel. When vendors went unpaid through 2025 fragrance houses began pulling or withholding inventory.

The result is empty counters in the very stores where American consumers go to discover and repurchase their favorites. A consumer who discovers a fragrance on social media and walks into a Saks to try it may find the tester gone, the counter understaffed, or the product simply out of stock. That moment of conversion is being lost and the longer this persists, the harder it becomes to recapture.

Market Analysis
The Consumer Has Moved On: Why Niche Fragrance Is Outpacing Legacy Luxury

Something has shifted in the way people choose what to wear on their skin. The era of the signature designer scent the Chanel No. 5, the Armani Acqua di Gio, the Dior Sauvage is not over, but it is no longer the whole story. A growing and increasingly influential segment of fragrance buyers has decided that smelling like everyone else is exactly what they are trying to avoid.

The numbers reflect it clearly. The niche perfume market is growing at a CAGR of over 14%, compared to just 2.7% for mass-market fragrances and surveys show that 63% of luxury fragrance buyers now actively seek personalized or unique scents over recognized designer names. Niche brands have gained 10 percentage points of category market share in recent years, while traditional brands have declined. In the U.S. alone, niche fragrance unit sales rose 11.7% in a single year.

This is not a passing trend. It reflects a deeper shift in how consumers understand identity and self-expression. Fragrance has become personal in a way it was not a generation ago something discovered rather than assigned, worn as a signal of individuality rather than status. For brands and importers operating in this space, the opportunity is significant. But so is the challenge: these are smaller houses, with shorter production runs, rarer ingredients, and supply chains that require a more hands-on, compliance-fluent import partner to navigate successfully.
